Small changes, big impact / छोटे बदलाव, बड़ा असर

Three smaller interventions focused on improving clarity, trust, and user action.

"The images feel too graphic."
Observation
Users frequently reacted negatively to graphic medical imagery on fundraiser pages.
Constraint
The same imagery performed strongly in ads, so removing it entirely would hurt acquisition.
Solution
Introduced blurred imagery on story pages for users arriving from ads, allowing them to reveal the image only if they chose to.
Result
Story conversions increased from 10% to 18%.
"What does sharing actually do?"
Observation
The share button on cart and thank-you pages had low engagement.
Constraint
Sharing could not compete too aggressively with donation flows for user attention.
Solution
Replaced a generic share button with a structured card showing sharing platforms, the expected impact of sharing, and clearer interaction cues.
Result
5× increase in shares while maintaining Average Transaction Size.
"Monthly giving feels like a big commitment."
Observation
Monthly giving adoption on equalall was lower than expected.
Constraint
Brand familiarity was still developing, making long-term commitment difficult for new users.
Solution
Introduced a "Give Monthly for 3 Months" option as a lower-commitment entry point.
Result
Monthly giving sign-ups tripled.

Post-donation

Most donation platforms treat “thank you” as the end of the journey. I treated it as part of retention.
RoleLed design across three post-donation surfaces
TeamPM, engineering, user research
Timeline~9 weeks across three projects [confirm dates]
ImpactPost-donation conversion 2% → 12%, Average Transaction Size +50%

Why post-donation?

Most crowdfunding platforms focus heavily on the transaction itself. The thank-you screen, updates flow, and campaign-ended page are often treated as secondary experiences.

But for Ketto, post-donation is where retention, repeat giving, and long-term trust are built.

I approached post-donation as a connected journey across three moments:

  1. Thank You → gratitude and next steps
  2. Updates → visibility into impact
  3. Fundraiser Ended → closure and redirection

I worked on all three surfaces sequentially, with each project informing the next.

Surface 1 — Thank You (~4 weeks)

Ketto had multiple thank-you page variants across products, each with different layouts, logic, and upsell patterns.

I standardized the experience into a single scalable framework with consistent components and behaviour.

Outcome

+17% increase in upsell conversion across thank-you pages.

Surface 2 — Updates (~2 weeks)

After donating, users receive updates about how their contribution is being used. The existing experience treated updates as passive communication.

I redesigned the page to strengthen continuity between the donor and the cause, while creating clearer opportunities for continued support.

Outcome

The updates surface became a new acquisition channel for SIP donations.

Surface 3 — Fundraiser Ended (~3 weeks)

When a fundraiser closes, donor intent often still exists — but the existing page acted as a dead end.

Donations from this surface were near-zero, effectively a dead end.

Before redesigning the page, I spoke directly with donors who had visited closed campaigns to understand what they expected at that moment.

One insight became clear: donors wanted closure before another ask.

My early concepts focused too heavily on redirecting users to new campaigns. The interviews showed that users first wanted to understand what happened to the patient, family, or cause they had supported.

The redesign prioritized:

  • campaign outcomes
  • beneficiary updates
  • impact visibility
  • contextual opportunities to continue helping

Upsell opportunities remained part of the experience, but only after users received emotional closure.

Outcome

What had been a near-zero surface is now driving a meaningful share of post-campaign giving.

The combined impact

Together, the three interventions improved post-donation conversion from 2% to 12% with increased Average Transaction Size by ~50%.

What I learned

Users are not always ready for the next action a business wants them to take. Understanding emotional context is as important as optimizing funnels.

Direct conversations with users surfaced insights that analytics alone could not.

MetaGo

Designing a six-month medical commitment from 0→1.
RoleLead designer, MetaGo
Team1 PM, 1 APM, 1 researcher (managed), marketing collaboration
TimelineAcquisition + post-login launched October [year]. App launched within the following 1–2 months.
Scope16 landing-page variants across 5 months · 6 monthly research rounds with 80+ users · 20-screen logged-in MVP · two app versions
DesignedAcquisition website + quiz, logged-in MVP, app experiences, research dashboards. Branding handled by another team.
ImpactLanding-to-signup conversion 2–10%, quiz completion 92%

What MetaGo is

MetaGo is a medical-first weight-loss platform built within Ketto.

Eligible users are prescribed GLP-1 medications and supported through doctor consultations, coaching, diet plans, and ongoing tracking across a six-month program.

The onboarding flow asks users for significant commitment:

  • ₹1,799 initial payment for lab work and consultations
  • ₹24,000–60,000 six-month program cost depending on treatment

Although incubated within Ketto, MetaGo operates as a separate product with a different audience, problem space, and product language.

My approach to 0→1 products

I begin by designing the ideal version of the experience before aligning with engineering and product constraints.

This helps the team make deliberate trade-offs during MVP planning, while maintaining clarity on the long-term direction of the product.

Chapter 1 — Acquisition

We initially launched multiple landing-page variants with different positioning and CTAs. Over time, the quiz became the strongest conversion mechanism.

The quiz helped:

  • pre-qualify users
  • establish medical credibility
  • increase user investment before payment

Outcome

  • 2–10% landing-to-signup conversion
  • 92% quiz completion

A language change that reduced friction

Early research showed that users were uncomfortable with the word "injection."

In practice, the medication used an auto-injector mechanism closer to an EpiPen than a traditional syringe.

We updated product language and visual communication to better reflect the actual experience, which reduced hesitation during onboarding.

When the problem was trust, not pricing

Among users who paid ₹1,799 and later dropped off, most cited concerns around GLP-1 medication itself:

  • side effects
  • fear of dependency
  • family opposition
  • doctor pushback

Price was not the primary barrier.

This shifted the focus from pricing optimization to trust-building and education.

The recommendation was to introduce targeted educational communication before doctor consultations, helping users understand the treatment model and address concerns proactively.

Chapter 2 — Activation

Once users entered the program, the experience included:

  • onboarding
  • scheduling
  • coach assignment
  • lab coordination
  • progress tracking
  • recurring engagement

A key focus during MVP planning was ensuring every screen led to a clear next step, even within a reduced scope.

Chapter 3 — The app

Research showed that most users wanted a dedicated app experience, particularly for:

  • meal tracking
  • progress visibility
  • structured engagement

I designed and shipped two versions of the app in quick succession, using AI tools to speed up synthesis, iteration, and prototyping workflows.

AI tools used across the process: Claude, NotebookLM, ChatGPT, and Gemini.

Chapter 4 — Ongoing research

Research became a core part of product decision-making.

Qualitative

  • Monthly user interviews
  • Sales-call analysis
  • 80+ users across active users, dropped-off leads, and ambassadors

Quantitative

  • Dashboards tracking acquisition, activation, and retention patterns

The two informed each other continuously: behavioural patterns in dashboards guided research questions, while qualitative findings shaped future product decisions.

Key findings

1. Weight loss was not the only success metric

Early users often focused on cravings and visible weight reduction.

Over time, users also reported improvements in:

  • sleep
  • blood sugar markers
  • energy levels
  • eating behaviour
  • overall health indicators

This led to changes in onboarding expectations and the introduction of non-scale progress tracking inside the app.

2. Coaching value changed over time

Users valued accountability and support early in the journey. However, by later stages of the program, some users began comparing MetaGo's cost against purchasing medication independently.

This highlighted the need to evolve the coaching model as user maturity increased.

3. Household behaviour affected adherence

Diet planning decisions often involved another family member responsible for meal preparation.

This influenced coaching protocols and communication strategies.

Additional outputs from the research process

The same research workflow also informed:

  • user segmentation models
  • sales narratives
  • content strategy
  • medication delivery improvements

Research was not a separate function from product design — it directly shaped roadmap and prioritization decisions.

What I learned

Designing 0→1 products requires clarity around priorities, constraints, and sequencing.

The business metric being optimized is not always the same metric users emotionally value most.

A new way to give

A small intervention that grew to 15% of GMV.
RoleDesigner
TeamPM, engineering, NGO partnerships
TimelineDec 2024 → March 2025
ScopeSingle-component intervention on existing food-and-hunger donation flows
Impact15% of total GMV within 6 months

The insight

During a donor research call, one user said:

"It feels nicer to feed a cow a banana than to donate online."

The comment highlighted a larger behavioural gap: online donation often feels intangible.

Donors wanted a clearer sense of what their contribution was enabling.

The intervention

The existing donation flow already performed well, so the solution needed to work within the current structure rather than replace it.

I introduced a single component on food-and-hunger campaigns that allowed users to donate toward tangible items instead of generic amounts.

Examples:

₹300 = 1 rice bag
₹500 = 1 vegetable kit

The underlying donation values remained the same, but the framing became more concrete and emotionally understandable.

The standard donation flow remained available alongside it.

Extending the experience

The intervention was integrated into the existing post-donation ecosystem:

  • Thank You pages confirmed item contributions
  • Updates communicated delivery and impact
  • Emails continued the same language throughout

Because the supporting infrastructure already existed, the feature could launch quickly without requiring major platform changes.

Outcome

Within six months, the intervention contributed 15% of total GMV.

The growth came from:

  • new donors entering food-and-hunger campaigns
  • existing donors shifting toward item-based giving

What I learned

Small interventions can create outsized impact when they align with existing user behaviour.

In this case, the challenge was not changing the donation flow entirely, but reframing how the contribution was understood.
